Understanding the Equivalence of Externally Studentized and Standardized LOOCV Residuals in Linear Models

An Illustration with R

Author

Longhai Li

Published

September 30, 2025

1 Introduction

In statistical modeling, identifying data points that don‚Äôt fit‚Äîthe outliers‚Äîis a critical step. The most reliable tool for this job is the externally studentized residual. Its power comes from a simple, intuitive idea: to judge a point fairly, you shouldn‚Äôt use that point when building your model. This is the core principle of Leave-One-Out Cross-Validation (LOOCV).

This article provides a complete walkthrough of this essential concept. We‚Äôll start with the basic linear model, introduce the necessary notation, explore the flaws of simpler residuals, and then formally define and prove the equivalence of the conceptual and computational formulas for studentized residuals. Finally, we‚Äôll make it all concrete with a simple example.

2 The Linear Model

Our discussion is based on the standard multiple linear regression model. In matrix form, the relationship between a response vector Y and a predictor matrix X is: \[ \mathbf{Y} = \mathbf{X\beta} + \mathbf{\epsilon} \] Where:

  • Y is an n x 1 vector of the observed outcomes.
  • X is the n x p design matrix of predictor variables (where p is the number of coefficients, including the intercept).
  • \(\beta\) is the p x 1 vector of unknown true coefficients we want to estimate.
  • \(\epsilon\) is an n x 1 vector of unobservable random errors, assumed to be independent and identically distributed with a mean of 0 and a variance of \(\sigma^2\).

3 Our Notations üí°

To discuss models fit with all data versus those with one point removed, we need clear notation.

3.1 Full Data Model (Using all n observations)

  • \(\hat{\beta}\): The estimated coefficient vector.
  • \(\hat{y}_i\): The predicted value for observation i from this model.
  • \(e_i\): The ordinary residual (\(e_i = y_i - \hat{y}_i\)).
  • \(\hat{\sigma}\): The estimated standard deviation of the errors (Residual Standard Error).
  • \(h_{ii}\): The leverage of observation i, a measure of how much its x-values influence the model.

3.2 Leave-One-Out (LOOCV) Model

  • \(\hat{\beta}_{-i}\): The coefficient vector estimated after removing observation i.
  • \(\hat{y}_{i,-i}\): The predicted value for observation i, from the model fit without observation i.
  • \(e_{i,-i}\): The deleted residual (\(e_{i,-i} = y_i - \hat{y}_{i,-i}\)).
  • \(\hat{\sigma}_{-i}\): The standard deviation of the errors estimated from the model fit without observation i.

4 Two Problematic Residuals

Before getting to the correct solution, it‚Äôs crucial to understand why simpler methods of standardizing residuals are flawed.

4.1 The Ordinary Residual (\(e_i\)): Biased (Too Small) and \(x_i\) Dependent

The most basic residual, \(e_i\), is problematic for two key reasons.

Systematically Too Small: An outlier has an undue influence on the model, pulling the regression line towards itself. This makes its own predicted value, \(\hat{y}_i\), artificially close to its actual value, \(y_i\). As a result, its residual, \(e_i\), is deceptively small and doesn‚Äôt reflect the true magnitude of the error.

\(x_i\)-Dependent Variance: The variance of an ordinary residual is not constant; it depends on the point‚Äôs leverage. \[ \text{Var}(e_i) = \sigma^2(1 - h_{ii}) \] Since leverage (\(h_{ii}\)) is always greater than 0, the variance of an ordinary residual is always less than the true error variance, \(\sigma^2\). High-leverage points act as ‚Äúanchors‚Äù for the line and have even smaller variance.

4.2 The LOOCV Residual (\(e_{i,-i}\)): Not biased, but too large, \(x_i\)-Dependent Variance

The deleted residual, \(e_{i,-i}\), solves the ‚Äútoo small‚Äù problem. Because the model isn‚Äôt influenced by the point it‚Äôs predicting, the residual is an honest measure of prediction error. However, its variance is still not constant.

\(x_i\)-Dependent Variance: The variance of a deleted residual also depends on leverage, but in the opposite way. \[ \text{Var}(e_{i,-i}) = \frac{\sigma^2}{1 - h_{ii}} \] Since \(1-h_{ii}\) is less than 1, the variance of a deleted residual is always greater than the true error variance, \(\sigma^2\). This is because it has two sources of randomness: the error in the point itself (\(y_i\)) and the error in the prediction (\(\hat{y}_{i,-i}\)).

5 Studentized LOOCV Residual

The correct solution is to take the honest LOOCV residual and divide it by its true standard error, which properly accounts for its larger, x-dependent variance. This is the externally studentized residual, \(t_i\).

Conceptual Definition: \[ t_i = \frac{e_{i,-i}}{\text{SE}(e_{i,-i})} = \frac{e_{i,-i}}{\frac{\hat{\sigma}_{-i}}{\sqrt{1-h_{ii}}}} \] This final value is a reliable diagnostic. Under the null hypothesis that the observation is not an outlier, it follows a Student‚Äôs t-distribution with n - p - 1 degrees of freedom.

5.1 Extenally Studentized Residuals ‚öôÔ∏è

Calculating the conceptual formula appears to require fitting n different regression models‚Äîa computationally expensive task. Fortunately, a mathematical identity allows us to calculate the exact same value using only the results from the single, full data model.

Computational Shortcut Formula: \[ t_i = \frac{e_i}{\hat{\sigma}_{-i}\sqrt{1 - h_{ii}}} \] This is not an approximation; it is an exact algebraic rearrangement of the conceptual definition.

5.2 Proof of Equivalence ‚öôÔ∏è

The proof that the conceptual and computational formulas are identical hinges on a key algebraic identity that connects the LOOCV residual (\(e_{i,-i}\)) to the ordinary residual (\(e_i\)).

Key Identity: \[e_{i,-i} = \frac{e_i}{1 - h_{ii}}\] This identity shows that we can find the ‚Äúpure‚Äù leave-one-out residual without actually refitting the model; all we need are the results from the single model fit on the full dataset.

5.3 The Derivation

Now, let‚Äôs start with the conceptual definition of the studentized residual and show how it transforms into the shortcut formula.

Start with the conceptual LOOCV definition: \[ t_i = \frac{e_{i,-i}}{\text{SE}(e_{i,-i})} = \frac{e_{i,-i}}{\frac{\hat{\sigma}_{-i}}{\sqrt{1-h_{ii}}}} \]

Substitute the key identity into the numerator: \[ t_i = \frac{\frac{e_i}{1 - h_{ii}}}{\frac{\hat{\sigma}_{-i}}{\sqrt{1 - h_{ii}}}} \]

Simplify the complex fraction. We can do this by multiplying the numerator by the reciprocal of the denominator: \[ t_i = \frac{e_i}{1-h_{ii}} \cdot \frac{\sqrt{1-h_{ii}}}{\hat{\sigma}_{-i}} \]

Cancel the terms. Since \(1 - h_{ii} = (\sqrt{1 - h_{ii}})^2\), one of the \(\sqrt{1 - h_{ii}}\) terms in the denominator cancels with the term in the numerator. This leaves us with the computational shortcut formula: \[ t_i = \frac{e_i}{\hat{\sigma}_{-i}\sqrt{1-h_{ii}}} \]

This proves that the two formulas are mathematically identical. The computational shortcut is simply a clever algebraic rearrangement of the more intuitive LOOCV definition, allowing for efficient and accurate calculation. ‚úÖ

6 Example 1: The Intercept-Only Model

Let‚Äôs make this concrete with the simplest model: \(y_i = \mu + \epsilon_i\). Here, the ‚Äúbest fit‚Äù is just the sample mean. We can demonstrate the equivalence of the formulas numerically in R.

library(dplyr)

# --- 1. Setup the Data ---
set.seed(42)
y <- c(rnorm(9, mean = 10, sd = 2), 20) # 9 normal points and 1 outlier
n <- length(y)
# Create a data frame for the lm() function
full_data <- data.frame(y = y, x = 1) # x is a dummy for the intercept

# --- 2. Brute-Force Calculation via Looping ---

# Create vectors to store our results
deleted_residuals_manual <- numeric(n)
studentized_residuals_manual <- numeric(n)

# Calculate leverage once from the full model, as it's needed for the studentized formula
full_model <- lm(y ~ 1, data = full_data)
leverage <- hatvalues(full_model)

# Loop through each data point
for (i in 1:n) {
  # Create the leave-one-out dataset by removing the i-th row
  loocv_data <- full_data[-i, ]
  
  # Refit the model on the reduced dataset
  loocv_model <- lm(y ~ 1, data = loocv_data)
  
  # Predict the value for the single case that was removed
  # We use the original data point as 'newdata'
  predicted_value <- predict(loocv_model, newdata = full_data[i, ])
  
  # Calculate the deleted residual (e_{i,-i})
  deleted_residuals_manual[i] <- full_data$y[i] - predicted_value
  
  # Get sigma_hat_minus_i from the summary of the new model
  sigma_hat_minus_i <- summary(loocv_model)$sigma
  
  # Calculate the studentized residual (t_i)
  h_ii <- leverage[i]
  studentized_residuals_manual[i] <- deleted_residuals_manual[i] / 
                                     (sigma_hat_minus_i * sqrt(1 - h_ii))
}


# --- 3. Compare with R's Efficient Shortcut Functions ---
efficient_deleted <- residuals(full_model) / (1 - leverage)
efficient_studentized <- rstudent(full_model)

# Create a comparison data frame
comparison_df <- data.frame(
  Observation = 1:n,
  Manual_Deleted = deleted_residuals_manual,
  Efficient_Deleted = efficient_deleted,
  Manual_Studentized = studentized_residuals_manual,
  Efficient_Studentized = efficient_studentized
)

# Print the comparison table

# Generate some sample data
set.seed(42)
y <- c(rnorm(9, mean = 10, sd = 2), 20) # 9 normal points and 1 outlier
n <- length(y)
p <- 1 # Only one parameter (the intercept)

# Let's focus on the outlier, which is the 10th data point (i=10)
i <- 10
y_i <- y[i]

6.1 Calculate the Components

First, we‚Äôll manually calculate all the terms we need for our outlier (\(i=10\)).

# --- Full Model Calculations ---
y_bar <- mean(y)
e_i <- y_i - y_bar
# For an intercept-only model, leverage is the same for all points
h_ii <- 1 / n

# --- LOOCV Model Calculations ---
y_minus_i <- y[-i]
y_bar_minus_i <- mean(y_minus_i)
e_i_minus_i <- y_i - y_bar_minus_i

# Calculate sigma_hat_minus_i
# Degrees of freedom for the LOOCV model is (n-1) - p = n-p-1
df_minus_i <- n - p - 1
# Residual sum of squares for the LOOCV model
rss_minus_i <- sum((y_minus_i - y_bar_minus_i)^2)
# sigma_hat_minus_i is the square root of MSE_minus_i
sigma_hat_minus_i <- sqrt(rss_minus_i / df_minus_i)

6.2 Showing the Equivalence

Now, let‚Äôs plug these components into both formulas for the studentized residual, \(t_i\).

# Conceptual Formula
t_conceptual <- e_i_minus_i / (sigma_hat_minus_i * sqrt(1 - h_ii))

# Shortcut Formula
t_shortcut <- e_i / (sigma_hat_minus_i * sqrt(1 - h_ii))

# Wait, the shortcut formula from the theory is identical to the conceptual
# formula once we substitute e_i with e_{i,-i}. Let's first verify Identity 1:
# e_{i,-i} = e_i / (1 - h_ii)
identity_1_lhs <- e_i_minus_i
identity_1_rhs <- e_i / (1 - h_ii)

cat("--- Verifying Identity 1 ---\n")

# are different. The full shortcut uses `rstudent()` in R, which also calculates
# sigma_hat_minus_i from the full model's results. For our purpose, let's just
# confirm our calculation with R's built-in function.

# R's built-in function for studentized residuals
model <- lm(y ~ 1) # Intercept-only model

As you can see, our manual calculation based on the conceptual formula perfectly matches the result from R‚Äôs optimized rstudent() function. The small example also numerically verifies Identity 1, which is the algebraic key to the equivalence.

6.3 Distribution of the Residuals

Under the true model (i.e., the observation is not an outlier), the calculated \(t_i\) statistic follows a Student‚Äôs t-distribution with \(n - p - 1\) degrees of freedom. For our simple intercept-only model, we are only estimating one parameter (p=1, the mean \(\mu\)), so the distribution is \(t\) with \(10 - 1 - 1 = 8\) degrees of freedom. Our calculated value of \(t_{10} \approx 4.5\) is extremely unlikely to come from a t-distribution with 8 degrees of freedom, correctly flagging it as a significant outlier.
